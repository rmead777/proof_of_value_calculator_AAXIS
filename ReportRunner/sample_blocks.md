## SKU Rationalization Analytics

### What It Does

SKU Rationalization Analytics systematically identifies which products in your catalog deserve investment, which should be managed for cash, and which should be exited entirely. The process combines sales velocity, margin contribution, customer concentration, and inventory performance data to segment your portfolio into actionable tiers — typically revealing that **20-30% of SKUs generate less than 3% of revenue** while consuming 15-25% of inventory capital and warehouse space.

Unlike periodic "SKU cleanup" exercises driven by gut feel, analytics-driven rationalization quantifies the *total cost of complexity*: not just inventory carrying cost, but the hidden burden on purchasing, warehousing, customer service, and IT.

### Impact Summary

| Category | Impact Level | Savings Range (Moderate) | Timeline to Impact |
|----------|--------------|--------------------------|-------------------|
| Inventory Carrying Cost | **PRIMARY** | 15-25% | 3-6 months |
| Returns/Obsolescence | **PRIMARY** | 20-30% | 3-6 months |
| Warehousing & Logistics | SECONDARY | 12-18% | 6-12 months |
| Order Processing | SECONDARY | 10-15% | 6-12 months |
| IT Costs | TERTIARY | 8-12% | 12-18 months |

### How It Works

The analytics engine ingests **24-36 months of transactional history** — sales, margins, inventory positions, and customer-level detail — and applies multi-dimensional segmentation. The output isn't a simple ABC analysis, but a decision matrix that accounts for strategic factors:

- Is this SKU critical to a key customer?
- Is it a loss leader that drives basket size?
- Is it required for regulatory or contractual compliance?
- What's the exit cost (liquidation, customer communication)?

For each rationalization candidate, the system models the exit scenario — enabling surgical decisions rather than blanket cuts that damage customer relationships.

### Expected Timeline

> **Time to Value**
> 
> | Milestone | Timeline | What Happens |
> |-----------|----------|--------------|
> | Quick Wins | 60-90 days | Exit zero-velocity, negative-margin SKUs |
> | Core Rationalization | 6-12 months | Structured portfolio segmentation |
> | Sustained Discipline | Ongoing | Governance for new SKU introduction |

SKU Rationalization offers a **dual timeline**: quick wins within 90 days from obvious tail-end cuts, and sustained value over 12-18 months as the organization builds capability for ongoing portfolio management.

The 90-day quick wins come from "no-brainer" cuts — SKUs with zero sales in 18+ months, items with negative margin when fully costed, duplicates from acquisition history. The longer-term value comes from institutionalizing rationalization as a discipline, preventing complexity from creeping back.

### Impact: SKU Rationalization → Inventory Carrying Cost

SKU Rationalization delivers **PRIMARY** impact to Inventory Carrying Cost — one of its two strongest effect pathways.

| Scenario | Reduction Range | Confidence |
|----------|-----------------|------------|
| Conservative | 10-15% | HIGH |
| Moderate | 15-25% | HIGH |
| Aggressive | 25-40% | MEDIUM |

**The Mechanism**

The cause-effect chain is direct: fewer SKUs means less capital tied up in stock, less warehouse space consumed, and less management overhead per dollar of inventory investment.

The typical distributor carries **20-30% more SKUs than necessary** to serve actual customer demand. These excess items — accumulated through acquisitions, customer requests that never materialized, supplier promotions, and simple inertia — each require safety stock, occupy warehouse locations, and demand purchasing attention.

> **The Math**
> 
> For a distributor with $20M in inventory:
> - Bottom 20% of SKUs = ~$4M in tied capital
> - Rationalization releases $3-4M in working capital
> - At 25% carrying cost = **$750K-$1M annual savings**

**Three Value Channels**

1. **Direct inventory reduction** — eliminating SKUs means liquidating current stock (one-time cash release) and stopping future replenishment (permanent working capital reduction)

2. **Safety stock optimization** — fewer SKUs means remaining items receive more forecasting attention and tighter safety stock parameters

3. **Velocity improvement** — counterintuitively, cutting slow-movers often *increases* sales of retained items as customers consolidate onto faster-moving alternatives

**Supporting Evidence**

P&G's $1B savings program included meaningful contribution from inventory carrying cost reduction as they pruned their portfolio. The distributor case study documented **$92K in carrying cost savings** just from the initial rationalization wave, with more projected as the discipline matured.
